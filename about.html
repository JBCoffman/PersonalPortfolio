About

Fractional CPO, Sociology graduate, Tampa Bay OG, Florida Certified BBQ Judge. These things are more connected than they appear.

Professional Narrative

I didn't start in product. I studied Sociology at the University of South Florida — which, as it turns out, is the most useful possible background for building software products. SaaS is mostly just understanding why groups of people do what they do, and then building systems that make the right behavior easier.

Fifteen years and a few inflection points later: I've scaled a B2B SaaS platform to $150M ARR, supported a $1.5B PE acquisition, launched an AI product that generated $14M in its first year, and built the governance infrastructure that made a NASDAQ company's AI investment defensible.

What I actually built in all of those situations — the thing that made the outcomes possible — was the operating system underneath the product. Strategy, governance, instrumentation, team structure. The unglamorous, load-bearing infrastructure that nobody puts on postcards. Mangroves, essentially.

I'm based in Manatee County, Florida. Tampa Bay OG. I work with a small number of companies at a time, because that's the only way to do this at the standard I hold myself to.

The Mangrove Principle

Mangroves are ancient, unglamorous, load-bearing infrastructure. The whole ecosystem collapses without them. Nobody puts them on postcards.

This is the work. Building the roots that make the visible outcomes possible. When the acquisition happens, when the AI product hits $14M ARR, when the compliance audit passes — it looks inevitable. It wasn't.

On cross-functional work

Product leadership is mostly organizational behavior.

Engineering, design, go-to-market, finance, the board — a product leader's job is to create the conditions where all of those functions move in the same direction at the right time. Sociology, again.

How I Think

Building SaaS is mostly applied Sociology.

I genuinely believe this. The hardest product problems aren't technical — they're behavioral. Why don't users adopt the feature you built for them? Why does the team keep solving the wrong problems? Why does the board keep asking the same question every quarter? These are all the same problem: you don't have a clear enough model of why the relevant group of people does what they do.

My background gives me a different diagnostic frame. I'm looking at incentive structures, group dynamics, and the gap between stated preferences and actual behavior. Most product leaders are looking at data and user feedback. I'm looking at those things too — and then asking why the data looks the way it does.

That shows up most in how I approach AI product work. The question isn't "what can the model do?" It's "what behavior change does this need to produce, in what group of users, and what does adoption actually look like at the org level?" That framing is why the AI products I've been involved with have generated real revenue — not just nice demos.

On AI Product Strategy

The companies that will win with AI aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones that correctly identify which behavior change creates the most business value, and then build AI that reliably produces that behavior change at scale.

On Operating Models

Strategy without execution infrastructure is just a presentation. Execution without strategy is just activity. The operating model is what connects them — and it's the thing most product orgs don't have.

Why I Do This Work

The unglamorous work is the interesting work.

I didn't get into product because I wanted to build apps. I got into it because building software products well is one of the hardest organizational problems there is — and most organizations are solving it badly.

The fractional and advisory model suits me because it keeps me close to the actual problems. Full-time executive roles have their own kind of leverage — but you also spend a lot of time in the same org, optimizing the same system. Fractional work means I'm constantly pattern-matching across different companies, industries, and stages. That makes me better at diagnosis, which is the most valuable thing I do.

The work that I find most satisfying is watching a product org that was in chaos start to operate with clarity. When the team knows how to make decisions. When the roadmap reflects a strategy, not a backlog. When the metrics tell a coherent story. That infrastructure is invisible when it's working — which means nobody celebrates it. That's fine. The outcomes speak for themselves.

What energizes me
  • Organizations at genuine inflection points — the messy, high-stakes ones
  • AI product problems with real monetization potential, not just demos
  • Talented product people who need better operating conditions
  • Companies where the product is good but the operating model hasn't caught up
  • Problems where Sociology and SaaS intersect — which is most of them
What doesn't
  • Roadmap theater — creating the appearance of strategy without the substance
  • Organizations where the real problem can't be discussed
  • AI investment without a coherent monetization hypothesis
